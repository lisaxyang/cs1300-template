This project explores how personas are used in UI design. A public interface is chosen in order to practice observations and interviews. From the gathered data, possible personas are drafted, and a storyboard is created to depict the persona interaction with the interface.
I hope you enjoy reading it; a lot of love was put into this piece. 😊


Chosen Interface:

Brown University Dorm Microwave in Young Orchard


Sketch of Interface:

Brief Description of Interface:

This interface allows users to quickly heat up food. It is adaptable to a busy lifestyle because it works effectively on simple foods and is generally automated so the user can leave and come back later. The interface has a column of buttons that are touchscreen, which control the time and power levels, and a tactile button to open the door in order to put food in and take it out.

Notes:

While sketching the interface, I wondered if the button to open the door would be problematic. It seemed that it could be a problem if you were holding something in your hand or that maybe over time the button wouldn't press as smoothly. I also noticed that the buttons were mostly touchscreen, and I noted that this would probably be important to make the interface more resistant to damage when exposed to so much food.



Observations 🔎


User 1:

This user uses the number buttons and presses them without much effort; they do not try to read the button screens and are holding utensils as they use it. They stop the microwave manually to stir the food, and restart the microwave instead of using the pause button. They press the buttons quickly, jumping directly from the number to start without pausing or hovering. They use both plates and wet paper towels to heat up their food. They also stop the microwave manually at the end. They do this after they hear popping noises and say "Okay, it sounds done". They pull out the food with their hands even though it is evident the container is slightly burning them.

User 2:

This user also has something in his hand while interacting with the microwave buttons. While the microwave is going, they are waiting by on their phone or filling up the water pitcher. They also leave the kitchen area to go to the bathroom or grab something from their room. They also race back to the microwave to turn it off before the sound goes off. They know there is a mute button, but think it is more fun this way. They also pull out the food with their hands even though it is evident the container is slightly burning them.

User 3:

This user does not have anything in their hands when using the microwave. They are reading the microwave buttons before they press anything, and when the microwave does not start, they spend a minute reading the screen and trying multiple button combinations. They expressed the fact that they were trying to heat it for 1 min 30 seconds, but adding 30 seconds caused the microwave pause and they just gave up and went for 1 minute and decided to check at the end if the food was warm. They wait by the microwave for it to finish, and at the end they check if the food is warm with their hands.


Notes:

I noticed that the button seemed easier to press than I thought, because two of the users were holding things in their hand (albeit light things) while pressing the button. It was also interesting to see that users would use sounds to check when to take out the food, or what they would do while waiting for the food. These things seemed related to how comfortable and experienced they were with the microwave.

Interviews

(Sample of) Prepared Questions

  1. What was your goal when you came to the microwave?
  2. How long did it take for you to complete that goal?
  3. Do you (strongly disagree, disagree, agree, strongly agree) that this is a reasonable amount of time?
  4. Which buttons do you use the most?
  5. Are there any buttons that you never use?
  6. Describe a time when the microwave did not do what you wanted it to do.
  7. Do you wish there was something the microwave could do that it canâ€™t right now?
  8. What are some typical meals you use your microwave for?
  9. Do you have time to clean your microwave?
  10. Have you ever heated some food in the microwave and forgot about it?
  11. Walk me through an ideal meal preparation.

User 1:

This user wanted to make dinner, and had gotten some corn for this week. They expressed that they hoped not to heat up the leftovers wrong and wanted it to be even, and hoped not to overdo the corn. They mostly use the buttons: 1, add 30 seconds, cancel. They ignore most of the other buttons. The user wished that the microwave could weigh your food and give you time estimate or could have more precise power settings, such as a dial. This user usually uses the microwave for leftovers from dinner. An ideal meal preparation for the user would be to have vegetables and meat already cooked or somewhat prepared so stir-frying them would be quick.

User 2:

This user wanted to heat up some water for tea. They expressed that as long as the microwave heats up the food they are okay with it, and that they really don't have much more expectations of the microwave. They mostly use the buttons: 1, 2, add 30; they do not touch nor know how the other buttons operate, they added humorously. In using the microwave, they emphasize the importance of beating the microwave to the beep (not letting it go off). They think the microwave is very reliable, and use it to heat up food for lunch or dinner when they are too lazy to cook. They also explained that they allow their cooking to be spontaneous rather than planning meals ahead of time.

User 3:

This user wanted to heat up some food for a snack. Although they had a problem with setting the timer for the microwave, they said that they strongly agreed that the amount of time it took to heat up their food was reasonable. This user only uses the number buttons and does not touch any of the specialty buttons, and says a microwave without them would be acceptable. When heating something up, they expressed an intention to continue monitoring the food in the microwave to make sure nothing goes wrong. The user, however, was confused why when trying to set the button the microwave somehow went to the clock settings. This user uses the microwave mostly for microwavable meals, as opposed to heating up leftovers. An ideal meal preparation for this user is a meal that requires no preparation.


Notes:

It seems like all the users agreed on mostly using just the number buttons in the microwave, even though they all had varying levels of experience with the microwave. It makes me wonder what type of users the other buttons are geared towards. Asking about what the users wished a microwave could do was a very enlightening question that I feel revealed not only how the user thinks about a microwave but also about their motivations and goals more than direct questions would.

Nervous Natalie

Nervous Natalie is a perfectionist college student. She is trying to make another meal for dinner and bought something she doesnâ€™t cook as often. This persona represents some college students who are already very strung out by school. They have always felt the need to be high achieving and live up to some standard, and of course that is not always possible. Therefore, this perfectionism leaks into other aspects of their life, such as food preparation. Like most college students, Natalie will probably need to use the microwave in her dorm from time to time to make food, and there are typically perfectionist-personality type people in high-achieving schools.

Notes:

In buildling the personas and the storyboard, it was most helpful to focus on what responses made each persona unique. This usually meant that what was most helpful were the conversations or answers that I was not expecting. For example, being able to understand the user's motivation and concept of the microwave through the question "Do you wish there was something the microwave could do that it canâ€™t right now?" was incredibly helpful in building the goals and feelings of the personas. In the end, building a consistent persona with focused yet clear goals informed the creation of the final storyboard.
